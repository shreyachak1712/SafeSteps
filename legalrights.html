Your Legal Rights

Empower yourself by knowing your rights under Indian Law

IPC Section 354 – Outraging a Woman’s Modesty

This section of the Indian Penal Code deals with assault or criminal force against a woman with intent to outrage her modesty.

  • Punishment: Up to 5 years imprisonment and fine.
  • Includes stalking, groping, verbal threats, or unwanted physical advances.
  • Women can lodge a complaint even if the act didn't involve physical harm but was mentally disturbing.

Zero FIR – File Complaint Anywhere

Zero FIR allows a woman to file an FIR at any police station, regardless of where the incident occurred.

  • Police are legally bound to register it and later transfer it to the appropriate jurisdiction.
  • Helps avoid delays in critical cases like rape, domestic violence, or stalking.
  • Prevents harassment of victims during jurisdiction disputes.

No Arrest After Sunset Without Magistrate's Permission

Section 46(4) of the CrPC states that no woman can be arrested after sunset and before sunrise.

  • Arrest during this time is only allowed under exceptional circumstances and with a written order from a Magistrate.
  • Even in urgent cases, female officers must be involved and safety measures ensured.

Right to Female Police Officer

A woman has the right to be interrogated or searched only by a female officer, ensuring comfort and security.

  • This is applicable under Section 51(2) of the CrPC.
  • Also applies during medical examination and custody situations.

Vishaka Guidelines – Protection at Workplace

The Supreme Court laid down these guidelines to prevent sexual harassment at the workplace.

  • Every workplace must have an Internal Complaints Committee (ICC).
  • Harassment includes verbal abuse, lewd comments, inappropriate physical contact, or coercive behavior.
  • Employees must be informed of their rights and complaint procedures.

Free Legal Aid and Helplines

Under Article 39A of the Constitution and Legal Services Authorities Act, women are entitled to free legal aid.

  • National Legal Services Authority (NALSA) offers support through District Legal Services Authorities.
  • Emergency helpline: 181 for women in distress, and 1091 for police-related help.
  • NGOs and legal NGOs also offer support in court representation and filing complaints.

Protection from Domestic Violence – DV Act, 2005

The Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act ensures safety from physical, emotional, sexual, verbal, or economic abuse.

  • You can seek protection, residence, maintenance, and custody orders.
  • No need for the woman to leave her home; she has a legal right to stay in the shared household.

Right to Privacy While Reporting Sexual Violence

Victims of sexual violence have a legal right to record their statement in privacy, in the presence of only a lady officer or a magistrate.

  • Under Section 164 of the CrPC.
  • The statement can also be recorded at home or hospital if the victim is unable to visit the police station.

Maternity Benefit Act, 1961

This act ensures that working women are entitled to paid maternity leave and other benefits during and after pregnancy.

  • Eligible women can get 26 weeks of paid leave for the first two children.
  • Also provides nursing breaks and protection from dismissal during maternity leave.

Right to Equal Pay

Under the Equal Remuneration Act, 1976, a woman has the right to receive equal pay for equal work.

  • No employer can discriminate between men and women in wages or work conditions.
  • Women can raise complaints with the Labour Commissioner or approach labour courts.

Medical Termination of Pregnancy (Amendment) Act, 2021

This amendment enhances the reproductive rights of women by increasing access to safe abortion.

  • Women can seek abortion up to 20 weeks with one doctor’s opinion, and up to 24 weeks for special cases.
  • Confidentiality of the woman’s identity must be maintained by doctors and hospitals.

Right Against Cybercrime and Online Abuse

Women have legal protection under IT Act Section 66E, 67, and 67A against online harassment, threats, and circulation of obscene content.

  • Cyberbullying, morphing, blackmail, and revenge porn are punishable offenses.
  • Complaints can be filed on cybercrime.gov.in anonymously.

Right to Register a Complaint Without Lawyer or Money

Every woman has the right to go to a police station and file a complaint without hiring a lawyer or paying fees.

  • No police officer can refuse to file an FIR, especially in sexual assault or harassment cases.
  • Under Section 166A of the IPC, refusal to file an FIR is punishable by law.

Right Against Child Marriage – Prohibition of Child Marriage Act, 2006

This act prohibits child marriage and allows women to annul such marriages after becoming adults.

  • Legal age for marriage is 18 for women and 21 for men.
  • Victims can file for annulment within 2 years of turning 18.
  • Those promoting or participating in child marriage can face legal punishment.

Right to Free & Compulsory Education – RTE Act, 2009

The Right to Education Act mandates free and compulsory education for all children aged 6–14, including girls.

  • Private schools must reserve 25% of seats for economically weaker students, including girls.
  • Education is a fundamental right under Article 21A.

Right to Protection Against Human Trafficking

Article 23 of the Constitution prohibits human trafficking and forced labor. IPC Sections 370 and 372 make trafficking a punishable offense.

  • Buying or selling minors for prostitution is punishable with imprisonment up to life.
  • Special laws like the Immoral Traffic (Prevention) Act, 1956 also protect women.

Right to Free Medical Care for Rape Survivors

Under Section 357C of the CrPC, all hospitals — public or private — are obligated to provide free medical treatment to rape survivors.

  • Doctors must not delay or deny treatment.
  • Survivors are not required to file a police complaint before receiving treatment.

Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace (Prevention, Prohibition & Redressal) Act, 2013

This law ensures women’s safety in professional settings.

  • Every company with 10+ employees must have an Internal Complaints Committee (ICC).
  • The Act defines sexual harassment and lays down inquiry and redressal mechanisms.

Right to Register Virtual Complaints

Women can file complaints through email, post, or even social media.

  • Police must record virtual complaints and investigate promptly.
  • This ensures accessibility for women who cannot visit police stations due to safety or location issues.

Right to Protection During Pregnancy – Workplace Rights

Pregnant women cannot be dismissed from jobs on the basis of maternity or health concerns.

  • Employers must provide light-duty work and cannot assign risky tasks.
  • Dismissal during maternity leave is illegal and punishable.

Right to Claim Maintenance Under Hindu Marriage Act, 1955

A wife (including separated or divorced) can claim financial maintenance from her husband if she is unable to maintain herself.

  • Applies under Section 24 and 25 of the Act.
  • Amount is decided by the court based on income and responsibilities of both spouses.

Right to Seek Protection from Dowry Harassment – Dowry Prohibition Act, 1961

This act criminalizes the demand, giving, or taking of dowry.

  • Women can file complaints under IPC Section 498A for cruelty related to dowry.
  • Dowry-related deaths are punishable under IPC Section 304B.

Right to Restrain Publication of Victim’s Identity – Section 228A IPC

This law prevents the publication of the name or any identifying detail of a rape victim.

  • Violation is punishable by imprisonment and fine.
  • Media houses, individuals, and institutions must maintain strict anonymity.

Right to File Complaints in Any Language

A woman can file an FIR in any language she is comfortable with; the police must assist with translation if needed.

  • This ensures that language barriers do not prevent justice.
  • Police stations must accept complaints in vernacular/local languages.

Protection Against Mental Harassment in Marriage

Under IPC Section 498A, cruelty includes physical as well as mental harassment by the husband or his relatives.

  • Includes verbal abuse, humiliation, emotional blackmail, and coercion.
  • Women can lodge a complaint even if there is no physical injury.

Protection Against Workplace Discrimination – Article 15 of the Constitution

No woman can be discriminated against in employment or public life based on sex, caste, religion, or place of birth.

  • Includes equal opportunity in public appointments and fair recruitment practices.
  • Applicable to both private and public sector employers.

Right to Parental Property – Hindu Succession Act, 2005

This amendment gives daughters equal rights to inherit ancestral property.

  • Applies regardless of whether the daughter is married or unmarried.
  • Gives daughters the same rights and liabilities as sons in joint family property.

Right to Seek Custody of Children – Guardians and Wards Act, 1890

In divorce or separation cases, a woman has the right to apply for custody of her minor children.

  • The court decides custody based on the child's welfare, not just gender of the parent.
  • Custody can be sole, joint, or visitation-based.

Right to Free Education in Government Institutions

Women from economically weaker sections can avail free or subsidized education in many government institutions.

  • Central and state scholarships are available for girls pursuing school or college education.
  • Single girl child schemes and SC/ST/OBC-specific scholarships provide extra support.

Right to be Heard in Court via Video Conferencing

Women have the right to request video conferencing for court hearings, especially in sensitive or trauma-related cases.

  • Helps avoid physical presence in hostile environments.
  • Common in domestic violence and sexual abuse cases.

Right Against Public Harassment – IPC Section 509

This section deals with use of words, gestures, or acts intended to insult a woman's modesty in public or private spaces.

  • Punishable with imprisonment up to 1 year and/or fine.
  • Applicable to eve-teasing, catcalling, or obscene gestures.

Right to Legal Representation in Family Courts

Women are entitled to a legal advocate while dealing with cases in family courts involving custody, maintenance, divorce, etc.

  • Can be represented by private lawyers or legal aid services.
  • District Legal Services Authorities provide free lawyers for eligible women.

Right to Register Domestic Abuse Under Section 498A IPC

Section 498A criminalizes cruelty by husband or in-laws, including emotional and physical abuse.

  • Includes dowry demands, torture, and harassment.
  • Punishable with imprisonment up to 3 years and fine.

Right to Protection under Article 21 – Right to Life with Dignity

Every woman has the constitutional right to live with dignity, safety, and personal liberty.

  • Article 21 is the foundation for many gender-justice decisions in Indian courts.
  • It covers reproductive rights, protection from violence, and freedom of choice.

Ban on Triple Talaq – Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Marriage) Act, 2019

Instant triple talaq (talaq‑e‑biddat) in any form—spoken, written, electronic—is illegal and void.

  • Punishable with up to 3 years imprisonment for the husband.
  • Aggrieved woman can claim maintenance for dependent children.

Right to Act in Self‑Defense – IPC Protection

Women have the legal right to defend themselves—including use of force—if an imminent threat exists.

  • If facing serious threats such as rape, kidnapping, assault or acid attack, the law protects self-defensive acts.

Right in Case of Stalking – IPC Section 354D

Legal action can be taken against repeated stalking or unwanted surveillance.

  • Includes following physically, monitoring online, repeated calls/messages.
  • Punishable with imprisonment up to 3 years.

Right to Immediate Medical Aid – CrPC Section 164A

Women subjected to sexual assault must receive free and immediate medical care.

  • Applicable in both public and private hospitals.
  • No requirement for prior FIR or police clearance.

POCSO Act – Child Sexual Harassment Protection

The Protection of Children from Sexual Offences Act (2012) ensures child-friendly legal procedures.

  • Immediate in-camera trials; identities cannot be disclosed.
  • Special courts and child-friendly victim support processes.

Women’s Reservation in Parliament – One Hundred and Sixth Constitutional Amendment, 2023

This amendment reserves 33% seats for women in Lok Sabha and state assemblies.

  • Applies after next delimitation exercise (expected by 2026).
  • Aims to enhance female political representation at national and state levels.

Dowry Prohibition Act, 1961 & IPC Section 498A

No one can demand or give dowry under any circumstances; harassment related to it is punishable.

  • Violators can face up to 5 years imprisonment and fines.
  • Dowry-related cruelty is punishable under Section 498A IPC.

Right to Free Transport for Pregnant Women – JSSK 2011

Under the Janani Shishu Suraksha Karyakram, pregnant women get free ambulance for institutional delivery.

  • Also includes free drugs, diagnostics, food, and stay in hospital.

Right for Unmarried Women to Adopt – Juvenile Justice Act, 2015

Single or unmarried women are allowed to adopt children under the Juvenile Justice Act.

  • This removes traditional barriers and enables women to become adoptive parents.

Criminal Law (Amendment) Act, 2013 – Nirbhaya Act Enhancements

Post‑Nirbhaya reforms expanded penalties for rape, stalking, acid attack, voyeurism, and marital rape.

  • Includes IPC Sections 354B–354F and stronger punishment frameworks.

Right to Not Be Subjected to Male Medical Examination

Any medical or physical examination of a woman must be conducted only by a woman or in her presence.

  • This protects dignity and privacy under various legal protections (CrPC, Evidence Act).

Right to Education – Article 21A & RTE Act, 2009

Every girl between 6 to 14 years has the right to free and compulsory education in a neighborhood school.

  • Private schools must reserve 25% seats for children from disadvantaged groups, including girls.
  • Education is a **fundamental right** under Article 21A of the Constitution.

Right to Non-Discrimination in Schools – Article 15

Girls cannot be denied education based on gender, caste, religion, or background.

  • Applies to admission, facilities, scholarships, and assessments.
  • Any discriminatory policy in government-funded schools is unconstitutional.

National Scheme of Incentive to Girls for Secondary Education (NSIGSE)

This scheme promotes secondary education among girls belonging to SC/ST or economically weaker families.

  • Eligible girls get ₹3,000 in fixed deposit, withdrawn after 18 years and passing 10th standard.
  • Meant to reduce dropout after class 8.

Scholarships for Higher Education of Girls (UGC, AICTE, Central Govt)

Numerous merit-based and need-based scholarships ensure continued education for girls in colleges and technical fields.

  • Examples: Pragati Scheme (AICTE), Begum Hazrat Mahal Scholarship (Maulana Azad Foundation), CBSE Single Girl Child Scholarship.
  • Girls from SC/ST/OBC/EWS also get access to Post Matric and Pre-Matric scholarships.

Right to Safe and Hygienic School Environment

Under the RTE Act and guidelines from the Ministry of Education, schools must ensure separate toilets for girls and safe transport.

  • Lack of sanitation facilities is recognized as a key reason for dropout among adolescent girls.
  • Schools must provide menstrual hygiene education and materials.

Right to Adult Literacy & Vocational Training – National Literacy Mission

Adult women who missed formal education have the right to free literacy and skill-based training programs.

  • Schemes like Saakshar Bharat and Disha offer basic education, digital skills, and livelihood training to women above 15.

Free Education for Single Girl Child (CBSE/UGC Schemes)

CBSE and UGC offer full tuition waivers or scholarships to girls who are the only child of their parents.

  • UGC’s Post-Graduate Indira Gandhi Scholarship offers ₹36,200 per year for 2 years.
  • CBSE-affiliated schools offer free tuition to single girl children in classes 6 to 12.

Residential Schooling Schemes for Marginalized Girls

Government supports girls’ education through free residential schooling under Kasturba Gandhi Balika Vidyalaya (KGBV).

  • KGBV provides boarding, uniforms, learning materials, and female teachers for girls from SC/ST/OBC and minority groups.

Right to Access STEM Education & Reservation in Institutes

Girls have access to reserved seats and fellowships in top STEM institutions like IITs, NITs, and AIIMS.

  • IITs reserve a minimum 20% supernumerary seats for female students.
  • Women-specific fellowships (e.g., DST's WISE Scheme, KIRAN) support post-graduate research.

Right to Report Cyber Stalking and Harassment – IPC Section 354D

Repeated online following, monitoring activity, or sending unwanted messages constitutes cyber stalking.

  • First-time offense: up to 3 years imprisonment and fine.
  • Second or subsequent offense: up to 5 years imprisonment.

Right to File Complaints Against Revenge Porn – IT Act Section 66E & 67A

Publishing or transmitting intimate images/videos without consent is a punishable cyber offense.

  • Section 66E deals with violation of privacy through electronic means.
  • Section 67A penalizes sexually explicit content shared online with up to 7 years in jail.

Right to Anonymity in Reporting Online Abuse

Women and minors can lodge complaints anonymously on government portals.

  • Visit cybercrime.gov.in for anonymous complaint filing.
  • Personal identity is protected by law during investigation and prosecution.

Protection Against Cyber Defamation – IPC Section 500 & IT Act

False information or slander spread online (social media, websites) is punishable as cyber defamation.

  • Victims can file civil and criminal cases.
  • Penalty includes imprisonment up to 2 years or fine or both.

Right to Block and Report Abusive Accounts

Under IT Rules 2021, social media platforms must allow users to report abuse and have a grievance officer to resolve it.

  • Grievances must be addressed within 15 days.
  • Users can request removal of impersonating, threatening, or abusive content.

Right Against Impersonation and Fake Profiles – IPC Section 416 & IT Act 66C

Creating fake online profiles, including using someone else’s identity or photos, is illegal.

  • Punishable by up to 3 years in jail and/or fine under Section 66C.
  • Victims can lodge complaints with Cyber Cell or online portal.

Protection Against Cyberbullying of Minors – POCSO Act & IT Act

Online bullying, trolling, or threatening minors is a serious offense.

  • POCSO Act applies if the content or messages are sexual in nature.
  • Parents, teachers, or guardians can lodge complaints on behalf of minors.

Protection Against Phishing & Online Scams – IPC Section 420 & IT Act Section 66D

Deceiving someone to obtain personal information (e.g., OTP, passwords) online is punishable.

  • Phishing, fake job scams, lottery frauds fall under this.
  • Section 66D provides for up to 3 years imprisonment and fine.

Right to File FIR for Cybercrime in Any State (Zero FIR)

Cybercrime victims can file a Zero FIR at any police station, even outside the location of the incident.

  • Police must register it and transfer to proper jurisdiction.
  • This right prevents delay in action and helps in digital evidence preservation.

Right to Digital Evidence Protection

Victims of online abuse have the right to ask the police to preserve screenshots, chats, videos, and call recordings as evidence.

  • Important for filing FIR and for court cases.
  • Police must document and maintain chain of custody.

Right Against Retrenchment During Maternity Leave – Maternity Benefit Act, 1961

Women cannot be dismissed or demoted during maternity leave under Indian labor laws.

  • Termination during maternity leave is illegal and void.
  • Legal remedies can be sought through the Labour Commissioner.

Right to Workplace Safety – Factories Act, 1948 & Occupational Safety Codes

Women have the right to a safe, hygienic, and healthy workplace environment.

  • Employers must ensure sanitation, ventilation, lighting, and drinking water.
  • Night shifts must include safe transportation and presence of female supervisors.

Right to Work from Home in Special Circumstances – Labour Law Reforms

Women can request remote work during maternity, illness, or safety-related issues.

  • Supported by Code on Social Security and OSH Code reforms.
  • Employers must accommodate justified remote work requests.

Right to File Anonymous Complaints at Workplace

Women have the right to report workplace harassment without revealing their identity.

  • Anonymous complaints can be submitted via email, portals, or suggestion boxes.
  • This prevents retaliation and encourages safer reporting.

Right to Resume Same Position Post-Maternity Leave – Maternity Benefit Act, 1961

A woman is entitled to return to her original or equivalent job role after maternity leave.

  • Any change in salary, position, or responsibility is legally prohibited.
  • Violation of this right can be challenged in labor courts.

Right to Crèche Facility at Workplace – Maternity Benefit (Amendment) Act, 2017

Organizations with over 50 employees must provide a crèche facility for working mothers.

  • Crèche should be close to the workplace or provided virtually if possible.
  • Mothers can visit the crèche four times during the workday, including nursing breaks.

Right to Grievance Redressal Committees – Industrial Disputes Act & POSH Act

Every company must set up internal mechanisms to address women’s complaints.

  • Includes Local Complaints Committee (LCC) and Grievance Redressal Committee (GRC).
  • Must include at least one external female member.

Right to Non-Discrimination in Promotion & Appraisal

Women must not be denied promotions or fair appraisal due to gender or marital/maternity status.

  • Protected under Article 15 and the Equal Remuneration Act.
  • Unfair practices can be challenged legally through labor forums.

Right to Awareness and Sensitization Programs – POSH Act, 2013

Companies are mandated to conduct regular gender sensitization workshops.

  • Posters, workshops, and digital training must inform employees of workplace rights.
  • Ignorance of policies is not an excuse in harassment cases.
